Fender Deluxe American Stratocaster HSS with Hot-Noiseless Pickups, DH-1 Atomic humbucker, S-1 Switching, Synchronized 2-Point Deluxe Locking Tremelo, Pau Ferro Fretboard, Locking Tune Heads in Candy Apple Red

My Fender is arguably the best guitar I personally own. The guitar was purchased about 15 years ago and was a very expensive guitar at the time of purchase. I knew somewhat early, as I started playing instruments around the age of 12, what I was looking for in a guitar. My first guitar was the Fender Squier Stratocaster, which I liked the feel and playability, but not the pickups. I realized fairly quickly that I was in search of a much “fatter” sound than that which a single coil pickup could produce. I have since since learned to appreciate the tonality of single coil pickups in the neck position, or bridge in a Telecaster. However, in the bridge position on my guitars to this day, I still prefer a high output Humbucker. 


When I was confident enough to make a significant jump to a “real guitar”, I wanted something I knew I would enjoy playing then and now. I liked the Stratocaster ergonomics then, and I still prefer Stratocaster based designs over Les Paul’s. This American Deluxe HSS “Fat” Strat has been my go to since the first day of purchase. The guitar is not a compromise in any sense of the term, as I had carefully selected the right combination of features I wanted. This goes all the way down to the color scheme of the instrument itself, which is absolutely beautiful. This is one of Fenders own logo’s signature color scheme, Candy Apple Red. You undoubtedly have recognized this guitar being played by Peter Townshend of “The Who”,  Andy Summers of “The Police”,  David Gilmour of “Pink Floyd”, John Frusciante of “The Red Hot Chili Peppers”, and Mike McCready of “Pearl Jam”, just to name a few.  The Fender Deluxe American Stratocaster HSS is loaded with Hot-Noiseless Single-Coil Pickups, a DH-1 Atomic humbucker, S-1 Switching, Synchronized 2-Point Deluxe Locking Tremelo, Pau Ferro Fretboard, Locking Tune Heads, and of course the premium finish. 


The guitar is extremely versatile, dynamic, and articulate by nature. Equally cable of playing every genre of music to a professional degree or perfection, it would be extremely difficult to find something to dislike about this guitar. I have always been a fan of Fender, specifically the Strat body design, as I believe this to be the most comfortable and practical guitar design in the history of electronic guitars. I am a fan of Gibson Les Paul models because of their unique sonic abilities, having developed a preference for “Super Strat” models by Jackson and Ibanez as they seem to offer the best of both. 

Gibson Les Paul Studio Premium Plus with. A high-gloss AAA flamed maple top, set '59 Les Paul rounded mahogany neck, 490R/490T humbucking Pickups, Custom Locking Tuning Heads, Custom Black Hardware, Upgraded Bridge

When it comes to Gibson, you immediately associate the brand with one guitar, the Les Paul. This is with good reason, as it is an excellent design, has an amazing tone, and is constructed with the utmost attention to detail and precision. The list of individuals who prefer a Les Paul is endless, and I’m not going to list them. Personally, it was probably Zakk Wylde and Slash that really made me want that to own one. Being that I’m not willing to spend $3,000+ for a guitar, a comprise had to be made. 

The comprise was found to be in purchasing the Gibson Les Paul Studio Premium Plus model. Gibson makes the studio model and markets it as a “cheaper model”. At over $1,500 dollars at the time (15+ years ago), it’s not really a cheap model. The model was designed to be just what it says, a studio guitar, not a stage guitar. It therefore omits several “flashy” elements that I believe make it look like an old man’s guitar to begin with. 


They justify the price because the guitar plays identical, and sounds identical, to any other Les Paul. Features that are not related to tonality are what are absent from this model, not build quality. This is actually preferable to me because features such as that god awful ugly pick guard really look bad on a modern day guitar. So what does it feature? Well it has high-gloss AAA flamed maple top, Mahogany Body, Gold Hardware, a set '59 Les Paul rounded mahogany neck, and “Modern Classic” 490R/498T humbucking Pickups. I installed custom black hardware, black locking tuning heads, black tune-o-magic bridge and saddle, and replaced the pickup selector with an EMG switch. Sound quality is good, it looks absolutely amazing, and it has an excellent neck and fretboard. Probably the most well made guitar that I own regarding fit and finish. The pickups are also very good for stock pickups, with both pickups producing excellent clean tones.  The 498T bridge pickup in particular produces excellent fat saturated tones with pronounced midrange. I know what you’re thinking, there sounds like a big “but” section on the way, and that’s partly true. 


What the Les Paul is not, is comfortable to play. The thickness of the guitar, the sharp corners, and very noticeable weight are serious downsides in my humble opinion. If any of these things don’t matter to you, then good for you, but they are universal across all Les Paul models. So if you’re in the market for a new guitar, and want a true Gibson Les Paul, expect these observations to hold true. 


Why does this matter? Well considering comfort and playability are two of the most important factors in determining whether you like any guitar, it’s kinda a big deal. It’s not the “fastest” guitar neck, and the upper frets aren’t as accessible as other guitar’s. Again, these factors do affect your overall ability to play, especially when you’re learning. These factors however aren’t necessarily a deal breaker, but it’s definitely a large factor influencing my opinion of the guitar. I do think it’s a fantastic guitar, but it’s not my favorite instrument to play. In summary it’s a great guitar for those who are already experienced playing, not beginners, or those willing to sacrifice playability for brand name and looks. 


Ibanez GRG Series GRG131DXBKF Matte Black and Red with Custom Locking Tuning Heads, Custom Pickup Guards, Custom Seymour Duncan Invader Pickups

I’ve always been a fan of Ibanez acoustic, electric, and bass guitars. I’ve almost purchased an Ibanez S series guitar on several occasions at guitar center. I did purchase Ibanez Artwood Series acoustic/electric awhile back and I’ve been very impressed with the sound quality, build, and playability. Recently I decided to purchase an inexpensive Ibanez GRG-131 series guitar to test the waters and decide if I was equally impressed. Because most people view Ibanez or Jackson as a “shredder” or metal guitar, they are still heavily underrated. The difference between “cheap” guitars when I started playing 20+ years ago and what’s currently available is striking. The Ibanez GRG that I bought is a really great guitar for $250. It’s equally capable of satisfying the demands of beginners and professional musicians. The same can not be said for my first guitar, which was a Fender Squier Strat, which only left me wanting more. 


The guitar has an excellent design, with Ibanez’s signature Wizard neck, which is a really a dream to play. The guitar has an amazing matte black and red finish which really stands out. They also happen to be my favorite colors, matte black specifically even. Its other weakness somewhat is the popular body, even though it isn’t as big of a deal as people think. It has a Maple top & neck, with a New Zealand Pine fretboard, which is actually very attractive. The body is a “Super Strat” style identical to Ibanez’s signature RG series that the brand is known for. The guitar required only one real modification to be acceptable to be competitive against guitars which cost $1,000’s more. They have to scarifies something at this price point and they really did, the pickups. The stock pickups in this guitar are, simply put, garbage. Ibanez calls them Infinity Pickups, and they are the some of the worst pickups I’ve ever heard, period. The are completely flat, very unclear, so muddy it’s hard to listen too. 


Thank goodness there is a solution that even-outs the playing field. Simply remove those “pickups” ASAP, install a pair of Seymour Duncan Invader Pickups, metal pickup guards, locking tuning heads, and your ready to rock. So that really makes the total price of the guitar around $500. Could you buy a regular RG or S Series guitar for that amount, surely, but they have equally disappointing pickups that you would want to replace. Unless you’re spending nearly $1,000 or more on an Ibanez, don’t expect that guitar to sound good with stock pickups. So think of this guitar as a custom Ibanez RG series guitar with amazing pickups. So in that context, I got a guitar that has become tied for my used guitar because of many factors. With those Invader pickups the guitar becomes an absolute shred machine. It looks great, plays great, and sounds great. At the end of the day, what more could you possibly ask for from a guitar at any price point. Furthermore it’s a great way to get a guitar that “chugs” like a demon for under $500.
